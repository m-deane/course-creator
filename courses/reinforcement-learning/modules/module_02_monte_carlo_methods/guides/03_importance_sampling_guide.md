# Off-Policy MC via Importance Sampling

> **Reading time:** ~14 min | **Module:** 2 — Monte Carlo Methods | **Prerequisites:** Module 1

## In Brief

Importance sampling (IS) allows Monte Carlo methods to estimate $Q^\pi$ (or $V^\pi$) using episodes generated by a *different* policy $b$. The key idea: reweight each return by the ratio of how likely the trajectory was under $\pi$ relative to $b$.

<div class="callout-key">
<strong>Key Concept:</strong> Importance sampling (IS) allows Monte Carlo methods to estimate $Q^\pi$ (or $V^\pi$) using episodes generated by a *different* policy $b$. The key idea: reweight each return by the ratio of how likely the trajectory was under $\pi$ relative to $b$.
</div>


## Key Insight

We cannot always generate data from the policy we want to learn about. Importance sampling provides a statistically principled way to correct for the mismatch between the data-generating policy (behavior policy $b$) and the target policy $\pi$.

## The Off-Policy Problem

**On-policy** (Guides 01–02): The policy we evaluate is the same policy we use to generate data.

<div class="callout-key">
<strong>Key Point:</strong> **On-policy** (Guides 01–02): The policy we evaluate is the same policy we use to generate data.
</div>


**Off-policy**: Separate the two roles:
- **Behavior policy** $b$: generates the data (explores freely)
- **Target policy** $\pi$: the policy we want to evaluate or optimize

**Why off-policy matters:**

1. **Exploration without contamination.** $b$ can be highly exploratory (e.g., uniform random) while $\pi$ learns to be greedy.

2. **Reuse of historical data.** Episodes from an old policy, human demonstrations, or a simulator can train a new policy.

3. **Learning multiple targets.** One stream of experience from $b$ can simultaneously train many target policies $\pi_1, \pi_2, \ldots$

4. **Q-learning and DQN** are off-policy — this is foundational to modern RL.

## Formal Setup

**Assumption (coverage):** For all states $s$, actions $a$, and time steps:

<div class="callout-info">
<strong>Info:</strong> **Assumption (coverage):** For all states $s$, actions $a$, and time steps:

$$\pi(a \mid s) > 0 \implies b(a \mid s) > 0$$

If $\pi$ would take an action, $b$ must also take it with positive probabil...
</div>


$$\pi(a \mid s) > 0 \implies b(a \mid s) > 0$$

If $\pi$ would take an action, $b$ must also take it with positive probability. Otherwise, $b$ never generates trajectories for parts of $\pi$'s support and we cannot estimate $Q^\pi$ there.

**Coverage does not require $b = \pi$.** The behavior policy can assign different probabilities, as long as it assigns positive probability wherever $\pi$ does.

**Notation:**
| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $\pi$ | Target policy (what we want to evaluate/optimize) |
| $b$ | Behavior policy (what generates episodes) |
| $T(t)$ | First termination time after time $t$ |
| $\mathcal{T}(s)$ | Set of all time steps at which state $s$ is visited |

## The Importance Sampling Ratio

Consider a trajectory segment from time $t$ to $T-1$:

<div class="callout-warning">
<strong>Warning:</strong> Consider a trajectory segment from time $t$ to $T-1$:

$$A_t, S_{t+1}, A_{t+1}, \ldots, S_{T-1}, A_{T-1}, S_T$$

The probability of this trajectory under policy $\pi$ and transitions $p$ is:

$$\Pr_\p...
</div>


$$A_t, S_{t+1}, A_{t+1}, \ldots, S_{T-1}, A_{T-1}, S_T$$

The probability of this trajectory under policy $\pi$ and transitions $p$ is:

$$\Pr_\pi = \prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \pi(A_k \mid S_k)\, p(S_{k+1} \mid S_k, A_k)$$

The probability under behavior policy $b$:

$$\Pr_b = \prod_{k=t}^{T-1} b(A_k \mid S_k)\, p(S_{k+1} \mid S_k, A_k)$$

The **importance sampling ratio** is their ratio:

$$\rho_{t:T-1} = \frac{\Pr_\pi}{\Pr_b} = \prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\pi(A_k \mid S_k)}{b(A_k \mid S_k)}$$

The environment transition probabilities $p(S_{k+1} \mid S_k, A_k)$ cancel. Only the policy probabilities matter.

---

## Why the Ratio Corrects the Bias

We want to estimate:

<div class="callout-insight">
<strong>Insight:</strong> We want to estimate:

$$V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t \mid S_t = s]$$

But we sample from $b$, so we observe $\mathbb{E}_b[G_t \mid S_t = s]$ (a different quantity).
</div>


$$V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t \mid S_t = s]$$

But we sample from $b$, so we observe $\mathbb{E}_b[G_t \mid S_t = s]$ (a different quantity).

**Importance sampling identity:**

$$\mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t \mid S_t = s] = \mathbb{E}_b\!\left[\rho_{t:T-1} \cdot G_t \;\middle|\; S_t = s\right]$$

**Proof:** Expand the expectation under $\pi$ and insert $\frac{\Pr_b}{\Pr_b} = 1$:

$$\mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t] = \sum_\tau G_t(\tau)\, \Pr_\pi(\tau) = \sum_\tau G_t(\tau) \cdot \frac{\Pr_\pi(\tau)}{\Pr_b(\tau)} \cdot \Pr_b(\tau) = \mathbb{E}_b\!\left[\rho \cdot G_t\right]$$

By sampling $G_t$ from $b$ and reweighting by $\rho$, we get an unbiased estimate of $\mathbb{E}_\pi[G_t]$.

## Ordinary Importance Sampling

Average the IS-weighted returns:

<div class="callout-key">
<strong>Key Point:</strong> Average the IS-weighted returns:

$$V(s) = \frac{\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}(s)} \rho_{t:T(t)-1} \cdot G_t}{|\mathcal{T}(s)|}$$

**Properties:**
- **Unbiased:** $\mathbb{E}[V(s)] = V^\pi(s)$
- **High vari...
</div>


$$V(s) = \frac{\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}(s)} \rho_{t:T(t)-1} \cdot G_t}{|\mathcal{T}(s)|}$$

**Properties:**
- **Unbiased:** $\mathbb{E}[V(s)] = V^\pi(s)$
- **High variance:** The product of ratios $\prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\pi}{b}$ can be very large or near zero, causing extreme weights
- Variance can be **infinite** when episode length is long and $\pi$ and $b$ differ substantially

**When it works:** Short episodes or $\pi \approx b$ (small ratio variance).

---

## Weighted Importance Sampling

Use the IS ratios as normalized weights:

$$V(s) = \frac{\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}(s)} \rho_{t:T(t)-1} \cdot G_t}{\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}(s)} \rho_{t:T(t)-1}}$$

**Properties:**
- **Biased** (but bias $\to 0$ as $N \to \infty$, so consistent)
- **Dramatically lower variance** — weights sum to 1, preventing extreme weight explosions
- Preferred in practice for almost all off-policy MC applications

**Intuition:** Weighted IS treats the $\rho$-values as relative importance of each return sample. High-weight returns count more; low-weight returns count less. The normalization ensures the effective sample size is always bounded.

---

## Ordinary vs. Weighted IS: Comparison

| Property | Ordinary IS | Weighted IS |
|---|---|---|
| Bias | Zero (unbiased) | Non-zero (consistent) |
| Variance | Can be infinite | Bounded, much lower |
| Convergence | Slower (noisy) | Faster (stable) |
| Extreme ratio behavior | Unbounded | Normalized away |
| Preferred for | Theory, short episodes | Practice, long episodes |
| MSE (in practice) | Higher | Lower |

**MSE = Bias$^2$ + Variance.** Weighted IS has lower MSE despite being biased, because variance reduction far outweighs the small bias.

## Algorithm: Off-Policy MC Prediction

**Input:** Target policy $\pi$, behavior policy $b$ (satisfying coverage), discount $\gamma$

**Output:** Estimated $V^\pi(s)$ for all $s$

```
Initialize:
  V(s) = 0 for all s
  C(s) = 0 for all s   # cumulative weight denominator

Loop for each episode:
  Generate episode using b:
    S_0, A_0, R_1, S_1, A_1, R_2, ..., S_{T-1}, A_{T-1}, R_T

  G = 0
  W = 1.0   # importance weight, starts at 1

  For t = T-1, T-2, ..., 0:
    G = γ · G + R_{t+1}
    C(S_t) += W
    V(S_t) += (W / C(S_t)) · (G - V(S_t))   # weighted incremental update

    W = W · π(A_t | S_t) / b(A_t | S_t)     # update ratio

    If W == 0:   # π assigns zero probability to A_t
      break      # ratio is zero; no further updates needed
```

The `break` condition is crucial: if $\pi(A_t \mid S_t) = 0$, the trajectory has zero probability under $\pi$ and contributes nothing.

---

## Algorithm: Off-Policy MC Control (Weighted IS)

For control, estimate $Q^\pi$ and improve. Sutton & Barto Algorithm 5.6:

```
Initialize:
  Q(s, a) = 0 for all s, a
  C(s, a) = 0 for all s, a
  π(s) = argmax_a Q(s, a) for all s (target: greedy)

Loop for each episode:
  b = any soft policy covering π (e.g., ε-soft)
  Generate episode using b: S_0,A_0,R_1,...,S_{T-1},A_{T-1},R_T

  G = 0
  W = 1.0

  For t = T-1, T-2, ..., 0:
    G = γ · G + R_{t+1}
    C(S_t, A_t) += W
    Q(S_t, A_t) += (W / C(S_t, A_t)) · (G - Q(S_t, A_t))

    # Update target policy greedily
    π(S_t) = argmax_a Q(S_t, a)

    # If behavior action doesn't match target, ratio = 0; stop
    If A_t ≠ π(S_t):
      break

    W = W · 1 / b(A_t | S_t)   # π(A_t|S_t) = 1 for greedy π
```

**The early termination** (`break` when $A_t \neq \pi(S_t)$) means only the tail of the episode where $b$ happens to agree with $\pi$ contributes to updates.

```python
import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict


def off_policy_mc_prediction(env, target_policy, behavior_policy,
                              gamma=0.99, n_episodes=100_000):
    """
    Off-policy MC prediction using weighted importance sampling.

    Estimates V^{target_policy}(s) using episodes generated by
    behavior_policy.

    Args:
        env:             Gymnasium environment (episodic)
        target_policy:   Callable state -> action (deterministic greedy)
        behavior_policy: Callable state -> probability array over actions
        gamma:           Discount factor
        n_episodes:      Number of episodes to generate

    Returns:
        V: dict mapping state -> estimated value under target_policy
    """
    n_actions = env.action_space.n
    V = defaultdict(float)   # weighted mean estimate
    C = defaultdict(float)   # cumulative weight denominator

    for _ in range(n_episodes):
        # Generate episode under behavior policy
        episode = []
        state, _ = env.reset()
        done = False

        while not done:
            probs = behavior_policy(state)
            action = np.random.choice(n_actions, p=probs)
            next_state, reward, terminated, truncated, _ = env.step(action)
            episode.append((state, action, reward, probs[action]))
            done = terminated or truncated
            state = next_state

        # Backward pass with IS correction
        G = 0.0
        W = 1.0

        for state, action, reward, b_prob in reversed(episode):
            G = gamma * G + reward

            # Weighted IS update
            C[state] += W
            V[state] += (W / C[state]) * (G - V[state])

            # Update IS weight: multiply by π(a|s) / b(a|s)
            # For deterministic target policy: π(a|s) = 1 if a == π(s) else 0
            if action != target_policy(state):
                W = 0.0   # this trajectory has zero probability under π
                break     # no further updates contribute

            W = W / b_prob   # π(a|s) = 1, so ratio = 1 / b(a|s)

    return dict(V)


def compute_is_ratio(episode_actions, episode_states,
                      target_policy, behavior_probs):
    """
    Compute the full trajectory IS ratio for a segment [t, T-1].

    Args:
        episode_actions: list of actions taken
        episode_states:  list of states visited
        target_policy:   state -> action (deterministic)
        behavior_probs:  list of behavior policy probabilities for taken actions

    Returns:
        ratio: scalar importance sampling ratio
    """
    ratio = 1.0
    for state, action, b_prob in zip(episode_states,
                                      episode_actions,
                                      behavior_probs):
        pi_prob = 1.0 if action == target_policy(state) else 0.0
        if b_prob == 0.0:
            return 0.0  # coverage violation — should not happen
        ratio *= pi_prob / b_prob
        if ratio == 0.0:
            break  # early termination once ratio hits zero
    return ratio
```

## Diagram: IS Ratio as Trajectory Reweighting

```
Behavior policy b generates:
  S_0 --A_0--> S_1 --A_1--> S_2 --A_2--> S_3 (end)
       b(A_0|S_0)    b(A_1|S_1)    b(A_2|S_2)

Target policy π would have chosen:
  S_0 --A_0--> S_1 --A_1'--> ...  (different trajectory)
       π(A_0|S_0)    π(A_1|S_1)    π(A_2|S_2)

IS ratio:  ρ = [π(A_0|S_0)/b(A_0|S_0)] × [π(A_1|S_1)/b(A_1|S_1)] × [π(A_2|S_2)/b(A_2|S_2)]

Reweighted return: ρ · G₀ is an unbiased sample of V^π(S_0)

If trajectory is unlikely under π but likely under b:  ρ ≈ 0  (near-zero weight)
If trajectory is likely under π and unlikely under b:  ρ >> 1 (high weight)
```

## Variance Analysis

**Ordinary IS variance** can be infinite (Sutton & Barto Example 5.5).

Consider a 2-action environment where $\pi$ always takes action LEFT ($\pi = 1$) and $b$ takes LEFT with probability $\frac{1}{2}$:

$$\rho = \frac{\pi(\text{LEFT})}{b(\text{LEFT})} = \frac{1}{1/2} = 2 \quad \text{per step}$$

Over a $T$-step episode: $\rho^T = 2^T$

For long episodes, $\rho^T$ grows exponentially. The variance of $\rho \cdot G$ becomes unbounded as $T \to \infty$.

**Weighted IS** clips this: the normalized weight $\frac{\rho}{\sum \rho}$ is always in $[0, 1]$.

---

## Variance Reduction Techniques

Beyond weighted IS, several techniques further reduce variance:

**Per-decision IS (PDIS)** (Precup, Sutton & Singh, 2000):

Instead of one ratio for the whole trajectory, use separate ratios for each reward:

$$\hat{V}^{\text{PDIS}}(s) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_t \sum_{k=t}^{T-1} \gamma^{k-t} \rho_{t:k} R_{k+1}$$

Each reward $R_{k+1}$ is weighted only by the ratio for steps $t$ to $k$, not the full trajectory. Unbiased and lower variance than ordinary IS.

**Control variates:** Subtract a baseline $b(s)$ from returns before IS weighting. Reduces variance without changing expectation.

**Doubly robust IS:** Combines a direct estimator with an IS correction — robust to errors in either the model or the IS ratio.

## When Off-Policy Matters

**Use off-policy MC when:**
- You have logged data from a historical policy and want to evaluate a new one
- You want an exploratory behavior policy but a greedy target policy
- You are learning multiple target policies from one experience stream
- The behavior policy must obey safety or operational constraints that the target policy wouldn't

**Use on-policy MC when:**
- No historical data exists and every episode is expensive
- $\pi$ and $b$ would need to be very different (high IS variance)
- Simplicity and stability matter more than data reuse

**Modern note:** Q-learning (Module 3) and DQN (Module 5) are off-policy methods that learn $Q^*$ from any behavior policy. They implicitly use IS ideas but avoid the explicit ratio computation through function approximation and replay buffers.

## Common Pitfalls

<div class="callout-danger">
<strong>Danger:</strong> The pitfalls below are the most common mistakes practitioners make. Each one can silently degrade your results without obvious errors.
</div>

**Variance explosion with ordinary IS**
The product $\prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\pi(A_k|S_k)}{b(A_k|S_k)}$ grows exponentially with episode length when $\pi$ and $b$ differ substantially. A single episode can have a ratio of $10^{10}$, swamping all other samples. Use weighted IS or per-decision IS in practice.

<div class="callout-warning">
<strong>Warning:</strong> **Variance explosion with ordinary IS**
The product $\prod_{k=t}^{T-1} \frac{\pi(A_k|S_k)}{b(A_k|S_k)}$ grows exponentially with episode length when $\pi$ and $b$ differ substantially.
</div>

**Coverage violation**
If $b(a|s) = 0$ for some $(s,a)$ where $\pi(a|s) > 0$, the IS ratio is undefined (division by zero) and the estimator is invalid. Always verify coverage before applying off-policy IS. A uniform random behavior policy provides universal coverage.

**Forgetting the break condition**
When the target policy is deterministic and $A_t \neq \pi(S_t)$, the ratio becomes zero and all earlier timesteps in the backward pass should get zero weight. Forgetting to break causes spurious updates using zero-weight samples.

**Interpreting WIS weights as probabilities**
The normalized weights in weighted IS are not probabilities in any rigorous sense — they are self-normalizing importance weights. They indicate relative importance of samples within the current batch, not absolute probabilities.

**Off-policy TD ≠ off-policy MC**
Off-policy MC with IS is conceptually clean but high-variance. Off-policy TD (Q-learning) avoids IS entirely by bootstrapping. They solve the same problem with different tradeoffs. Do not mix the IS correction technique from MC into naive TD without understanding the resulting algorithm (that's the basis of GTD and gradient TD methods).
